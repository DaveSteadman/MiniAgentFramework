### EU and India Finalize Landmark Free Trade Agreement
The European Union and India have announced a historic Free Trade Agreement (FTA) after nearly two decades of intermittent negotiations, marking a significant geopolitical shift in global trade dynamics. This pact, described by European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen as the "mother of all deals," aims to eliminate tariffs on most goods including chemicals, machinery, and electrical equipment. The agreement is particularly significant for the automotive sector, where duties on motor vehicles are set to drop from as high as 110% to 10% under a quota of 250,000 vehicles. For India, the deal provides preferential access to European markets for textiles, leather, and marine products, which are labor-intensive sectors heavily impacted by US trade barriers.

The timing of this agreement is heavily influenced by the aggressive trade policies of the United States under President Donald Trump. Both Brussels and Delhi view the pact as a strategic move to hedge against US unpredictability and economic coercion. India has been grappling with 50% tariffs imposed by the US, while Europe faces threats over issues like the Greenland acquisition proposal. By deepening ties, the two blocs aim to reduce strategic dependencies and strengthen supply chains, sending a clear message of cooperation in an increasingly fragmented global order. Von der Leyen emphasized that combining their economic strengths delivers security in an insecure world.

However, the deal is not without challenges. While negotiations have concluded, the formal signing is pending ratification by member states and the European Parliament, a process that could face hurdles similar to the recent Mercosur agreement. Legal experts warn that compliance with strict EU environmental standards and the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) remains a complex issue for Indian exporters. Despite these obstacles, the agreement is seen as a crucial building block for a bilateral relationship that spans nearly 25% of global GDP and a market of two billion people.

### EU Launches Investigation into Shein's Digital Practices

A primary concern is the previous sale of childlike sex dolls, which prompted reports from French authorities last year. While Shein claims to have removed these items and banned sellers, the EU is examining the systems in place to prevent such illegal content from appearing, as well as the transparency of the recommender systems used to suggest products to shoppers.

The probe extends to the "addictive design" of Shein's platform, including its rewards program and gamification features that may exploit user behavior. Under the DSA, Shein must disclose the main parameters used to recommend products and provide users with options that do not rely on data profiling. Commission spokesperson Thomas Regnier highlighted that while reward programs are not banned, opaque algorithms can manipulate consumer choices. The investigation allows the Commission to enforce fines of up to 6% of global sales if systemic risks are confirmed.

Shein has stated that protecting minors and reducing harmful content are central to their operations and claims to be cooperative with authorities. The company noted significant investments in compliance measures, including systemic-risk assessments and enhanced protections for younger users. However, the EU's scrutiny reflects a broader regulatory push to hold global tech platforms accountable for their content and safety standards. With $38bn in sales reported in 2024, Shein represents a major test case for the enforcement of digital regulations within the bloc.
